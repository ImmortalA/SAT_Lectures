Unit 14 — Quadratics

Quadratic equations are equations of the form:

ax2+bx+c=0(a≠0)

Their graphs are parabolas.

14.1 Tactics for Finding the Roots

The roots/solutions of a quadratic are the x-values where y=0.
Methods:

  1. Factoring
    Example: x2−5x+6=0
(x−2)(x−3)=0⇒x=2,3
  1. Quadratic Formula
    For ax2+bx+c=0:
x=−b±b2−4ac2a
  1. Completing the Square (see below).

14.2 Completing the Square

Transform quadratic into vertex form.

Example 1
Solve x2+6x+5=0 by completing the square.

x2+6x+5=(x2+6x+9)−9+5=(x+3)2−4

Set =0:

(x+3)2=4⇒x=−3±2

So x=−1,−5.

14.3 The Vertex and Vertex Form

A parabola’s vertex is given by:

x=−b2a,y=f(−b2a)

Vertex form:

y=a(x−h)2+k

where (h,k) = vertex.

Example 2

y=x2−4x+1

Vertex: x=42=2.

y=(2)2−4(2)+1=4−8+1=−3

.
Vertex = (2,−3).

14.4 The Discriminant

The discriminant is inside quadratic formula:

Δ=b2−4ac
  • Δ>0 → 2 real roots
  • Δ=0 → 1 real root (double root)
  • Δ<0 → no real roots (complex roots)

Example 3

x2+2x+5=0

, Δ=22−4(1)(5)=4−20=−16<0

→ no real solutions.

14.5 Quadratic Models

Quadratics model motion, area, profit, etc.

Example 4
Height of ball: h=−16t2+32t+48 (feet).
- Vertex gives maximum height.

t=−b2a=−322(−16)=1

.

h=−16(1)2+32(1)+48=64

.
Max height = 64 ft at t=1 sec.

14.6 SAT Tips

  • Always check if factoring is quick.
  • Memorize quadratic formula.
  • Discriminant saves time for “how many solutions” questions.
  • Parabolas open upward if a>0, downward if a<0.
