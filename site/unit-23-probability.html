Unit 23 — Probability

Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring.
On the SAT, questions focus on basic probability rules, counting, and simple cases.

23.1 Basic Probability

P(E)=Number of favorable outcomesTotal outcomes
  • 0≤P(E)≤1
  • P(certain event)=1
  • P(impossible event)=0

Example 1
A fair die is rolled. What is P(rolling a 4)?

P=16

23.2 Complementary Probability

P(not E)=1−P(E)

Example 2
Probability of rolling a 6 on a die is 16.
Probability of not rolling a 6 is:

1−16=56

23.3 Independent Events

Events are independent if outcome of one does not affect the other.

P(A and B)=P(A)·P(B)

Example 3
Flip a coin and roll a die.

P(Heads and a 3)=12·16=112

23.4 Mutually Exclusive Events

Events that cannot both occur.

P(A or B)=P(A)+P(B)

Example 4
Pick a card from a deck. P(Ace or King):

452+452=852=213

23.5 Probability with Counting

Use combinations or listing.

Example 5
From 5 students (A,B,C,D,E), 1 is chosen at random.
Probability it’s A or B:

25

23.6 SAT Tips

  • Decide if events are independent or mutually exclusive.
  • Watch for “at least one” → use complement rule.
  • In card/dice questions, always confirm total outcomes.
  • Reduce fractions!

Practice Problems (Unit 23)

1) A fair coin is flipped. Find P(Heads).
2) A bag has 3 red, 2 blue marbles. Pick one at random. P(Blue)?
3) Roll two dice. What’s P(sum = 7)?
4) A card is drawn from a deck. Find P(spade or heart).
5) What is the probability of not rolling a 5 on a fair die?

Solutions

1) P=12.

2) P=25.

3) Favorable outcomes: (1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1) → 6 total.
Total outcomes = 36.

P=636=16

4) Spades = 13, Hearts = 13.

P=2652=12

5) 1−16=56.
