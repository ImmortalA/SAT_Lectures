Unit 26 — Volume

On the SAT, volume problems usually test your knowledge of 3D geometric formulas and your ability to apply them in word problems.
Common figures: prisms, cylinders, pyramids, cones, and spheres.

26.1 Volume of Prisms and Cylinders

  • Prism (rectangular box, etc.):
V=Base Area×Height
  • Cylinder:
V=πr2h

Example 1
A cylinder has radius 3 and height 10.

V=π(32)(10)=90π

26.2 Volume of Pyramids and Cones

  • Pyramid:
V=13(Base Area)(Height)
  • Cone:
V=13πr2h

Example 2
A cone with radius 4 and height 9:

V=13π(42)(9)=48π

26.3 Volume of a Sphere

V=43πr3

Example 3
A sphere with radius 5:

V=43π(53)=5003π

26.4 Composite Solids

Sometimes shapes are combined or parts are removed.
Strategy: Add or subtract volumes.

Example 4
A cylinder with r=3,h=10 is filled with a cone (r=3,h=10).
Volume difference = πr2h−13πr2h=23πr2h

=23π(9)(10)=60π

26.5 SAT Tips

  • Always confirm if radius or diameter is given.
  • Watch units! SAT may ask in cubic centimeters, etc.
  • If asked about volume change, compare ratios instead of recalculating everything.
  • Example: If radius doubles, sphere’s volume increases by factor 23=8.

Practice Problems (Unit 26)

1) Find the volume of a rectangular box with dimensions 4×5×6.

2) A sphere has radius 7. Find its volume.

3) A cone has diameter 10 and height 12. Find its volume.

4) A cylinder has radius 5 and height 20. Find its volume.

5) If the radius of a sphere doubles, by what factor does its volume increase?

Solutions

1) V=4×5×6=120.

2) V=43π(73)=13723π.

3) Radius = 5.

V=13π(52)(12)=100π

4) V=π(52)(20)=500π.

5) Volume increases by 23=8 times.
